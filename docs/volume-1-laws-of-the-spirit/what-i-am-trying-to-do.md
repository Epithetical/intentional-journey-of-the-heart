# Introduction: What I Am Trying to Do Here


## The Starting Question

I want to begin with a question that has driven me for over two decades: what if the way the world of the Spirit works is just as logical and discoverable as the laws of physics? Not identical to physics, but equally orderly, equally real, and equally open to investigation by someone willing to pay attention.
Jesus rebuked Nicodemus in John 3, and His rebuke was not about a lack of piety or devotion. Nicodemus was a ruler of the Jews, a serious student of scripture. The rebuke was that he should have been able to understand how things worked in the Spirit, and he didn’t. That stings a little, and it should. If the teacher of Israel should have known, then surely, we who have the full canon, the indwelling Holy Spirit, and two thousand years of the church’s experience, should be able to see deeper than we do.
That is the starting point. Not arrogance, actually the opposite. It is humility in the face of how much we have been given and how little we have understood.
My initial starting point is the observation that we live a life so far short of what Jesus invites us into. A life of walking in the Spirit, of miracles in the natural, and living in intimate conversation with him.

## The Four Foundational Principles

Before I get into any of the specific explorations, I want to lay out the four principles I treat as axioms for this whole investigation. I borrowed the axiom language from mathematics: just as Euclidean geometry works from a small set of starting assumptions and builds outward, I am working from four starting assumptions that I hold at roughly 90% certainty. Further exploration may refine or deepen these, just as Einstein’s work refined Newton without discarding him. That is fine with me. These are working axioms, not dogma.
Laws of the Spirit exist. The spiritual world operates according to regular, predictable, lawful relationships; as orderly as the physical world, because the same God created both.
These Laws are knowable. We will not see all of them, but we should be able to see more than we do today. The wind blows where it wishes, but we should be able to see more than just the sound of it passing. In fact, we should be carried by it, following its direction and sustained by it.
These Laws are knowable in all three “worlds” simultaneously, in my spirit, in my mind and emotions, and in my body and actions. If I have truly grasped a law of the Spirit, my doing will change. Otherwise, I have only memorized it, which is different. Said another way, if I am not changed by God’s word, then I haven’t yet heard God’s word.
We can operate in these Laws. This is not merely an intellectual exercise. The goal is to live closer to the life Jesus modeled, where miracles are not the exception but the expected fruit of operating in alignment with how God’s world and kingdom actually work.
A note on how I am reading scripture in what follows. The Eight Explorations use a proof-text method — I identify specific passages with explicit causal structure and draw out the logical relationships. This is appropriate for law-identification. But Dallas Willard’s reading of the Sermon on the Mount in The Divine Conspiracy offers a complementary lens I want to name: he argues that Matthew 5–7 is not a collection of individual ethical demands but Jesus’s most comprehensive integrated description of how kingdom life actually works — the whole thing, held together, as a picture of the life Jesus is inviting us into. I recommend reading the Sermon on the Mount alongside the Eight Explorations as a reality check: if a proposed law does not fit coherently within the Sermon on the Mount’s overall picture of kingdom life, that tension is worth examining. And if all eight laws together do not begin to describe the life the Sermon on the Mount is pointing toward, the exploration is not finished. The Sermon is the target; the laws are attempts to describe its structure.
