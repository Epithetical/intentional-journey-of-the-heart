# Section 3: Individual Spiritual Formation Testing Protocol


## Starting with the Person in the Room

Every research program about spiritual formation has to make a choice about its unit of analysis. You can study churches, denominations, small groups, or individuals. The LotS project starts with the individual — not because community does not matter (it matters enormously, and Section 4 addresses small group testing directly), but because the heart that is being formed is a single heart. The Holy Spirit convicts one person at a time. The word of Christ lands in one person's hearing at a time. The Affective Taxonomy traces a journey that one person makes, stage by stage, even when they make it in the company of others.
The individual testing protocol is also where the LotS project stays most honest about what it can and cannot claim. A person's spiritual formation is complex, non-linear, and influenced by factors that no research design can fully account for: their family of origin, their prior experience of church, the quality of their current relationships, the trials they are walking through, the particular voice and character of the Spirit's work in their specific life. The protocol proposed here does not pretend to control for all of those variables. It is designed to observe the journey closely enough, over a long enough period of time, that patterns can emerge — and to capture both the patterns and the texture of what the journey actually feels like from the inside.

## 3a. Defining the Testing Unit

The individual testing unit for the LotS spiritual formation protocol is a person who meets four criteria. First, they are a self-identified Christian who takes the spiritual life seriously — not necessarily a mature or advanced disciple, but someone who genuinely wants to grow. The testing protocol is not designed for people who are indifferent to formation; it is designed to observe and support formation in people who have at least a Stage 1 or Stage 2 relationship to the journey (Receiving or Responding on the Affective Taxonomy). Second, they are willing to participate in a PROAPT small group meeting weekly for a minimum of twelve months. Third, they are willing to complete regular self-assessments and to allow their progress to be observed and documented. And fourth, they are willing to identify an external witness — a family member, close friend, or coworker who knows them well — who can be interviewed at the beginning and end of the study period.
The twelve-month minimum is important and deliberate. Stage transitions on the Affective Taxonomy are not quick events — they are more like tectonic shifts than lightning strikes. The research at Willow Creek (Hawkins & Parkinson, 2011) found that movement between spiritual growth stages typically takes years, not months, in the absence of structured formation support. The hypothesis of the LotS testing program is that the PROAPT model, applied consistently in a supportive community, can accelerate that movement — but "accelerated" in this context still means observable change over twelve months, not overnight transformation. The protocol is designed to be long enough to capture real movement while remaining feasible for church-based research.

## 3b. Assessing the Starting State

Before the formation process begins, each participant completes a baseline assessment designed to establish their starting position on four dimensions. These dimensions are not independent of each other — growth in one tends to support growth in the others — but each captures something distinct, and tracking them separately over time allows the research to detect which aspects of formation are progressing, which are lagging, and where the next growth edge lies.
**Dimension 1: Affective Taxonomy Stage**
The baseline Affective Taxonomy stage is assessed through a combination of self-report and facilitator observation. The self-report component draws from the Personal Spiritual Formation Assessment (PSFA), which is drafted in Appendix A of this volume. The PSFA asks the participant to respond to stage-characteristic behavioral statements — statements that describe what a person at each stage of the Affective Taxonomy typically does, says, and values — and to rate their degree of honest recognition in each. The facilitator observation component adds an outside perspective: the group facilitator, using the Group Affective Taxonomy Assessment described in Section 4, documents their initial impression of each participant's stage based on their first few sessions of observation.
Determining a person's starting stage is an interpretive act, not a precise measurement. Most people are not purely at one stage; they show behaviors characteristic of multiple adjacent stages. The practice in the LotS protocol is to identify the highest stage at which the participant shows consistent, habitual patterns — not occasional flickers — and to record that as the baseline stage. Movement is then defined as the emergence of consistent, habitual patterns at the next stage.


**Dimension 2: Scripture Trust**
The baseline Scripture Trust Index (STI-A, adapted from Campbell et al., 2021) measures where the participant sits on the trust continuum — from no trust in scripture as authoritative or reliable, through intellectual acknowledgment without personal application, through active engagement and partial trust, to full integration of scripture as the organizing principle of life and worldview. The STI-A is a 12-item self-report instrument; its draft is included in Appendix A.
The trust dimension is particularly important to establish at baseline because it predicts the participant's capacity to engage productively with the PROAPT process. A person at the low-trust end of the continuum can still participate in the group and benefit from the community — but the specific mechanism of Romans 10:17 (faith coming from hearing the word of Christ) will only begin to operate as trust in the word develops. Baseline STI-A scores help the facilitator calibrate their approach: a group with several low-trust participants needs more time in the early PROAPT stages (Pray, Read, Observe) and more patience with questions before moving to Apply.
**Dimension 3: Image of God Reflection**
The Image of God 4Rs Self-Assessment asks the participant to rate their current experience of each of Kilner's (2015) four dimensions of reflection: Reason (the mind brought into alignment with God's truth), Rulership (responsible stewardship of their sphere of influence), Relationship (depth of connection with God and others), and Righteousness (habitual alignment of character with God's character). The instrument is designed to produce a simple profile — a snapshot of where the participant currently sees themselves growing and where they feel most distant from the reflection of Christ in their life.
This baseline profile serves two purposes. First, it gives the participant and facilitator a common language for discussing formation goals — rather than the vague aspiration to "grow spiritually," the 4Rs framework invites a more specific conversation: which dimension is most alive in this person right now, and which is most dormant? Second, it provides an endpoint comparison at twelve months: has the profile shifted? Which R has shown the most growth? Does the pattern of growth map onto what the Affective Taxonomy stages would predict?
**Dimension 4: Spiritual Disciplines Frequency**
The Spiritual Disciplines Frequency Log (SDFL) establishes a baseline count of the participant's current engagement with key formation practices: personal bible reading (frequency and duration), personal prayer (frequency and character — conversational, structured, contemplative), participation in corporate worship, service to others, and fasting or other ascetic practices. This is the most straightforwardly quantitative of the four baseline dimensions, and it provides the context for interpreting changes in the other three.
The SDFL is not designed to assess the quality of these practices — a person can read their bible every day and still be at Stage 2 of the Affective Taxonomy if the reading is purely cognitive and the word is never landing in the heart. But frequency data is a useful starting point, and it becomes more meaningful when correlated with the other dimensions over time. A participant who shows significant stage progression without any change in discipline frequency is saying something interesting about the mechanism of formation. A participant whose discipline frequency doubles with no corresponding stage progression is saying something equally interesting.

## 3c. The Primary Outcome Variables

The testing protocol tracks four primary outcome variables over the twelve-month period. They correspond to the four dimensions assessed at baseline, and together they provide a multi-dimensional picture of individual spiritual formation progress.
**Outcome Variable 1: Affective Taxonomy Stage Progression**
The primary outcome variable is measurable movement from one Affective Taxonomy stage to the next, or identifiable deepening within a stage. Not every participant will show a full stage transition in twelve months — particularly participants who begin at Stage 3 or Stage 4, where the transitions are deeper, slower, and more costly. But every participant should show some movement: either a stage transition, a deepening of habitual behaviors within their current stage, or the emergence of Stage N+1 behaviors alongside their current Stage N baseline.
Stage transitions are identified by the co-occurrence of three indicators: (1) the participant themselves reports, in their Narrative Reflection Protocol entries, a shift in how scripture lands — not just more interesting but more personally binding; (2) the facilitator observes a change in the participant's group behavior — specifically, the kind of sharing they are willing to do, the personal risks they take in applying the word, the way they speak about God; and (3) an external witness reports observable changes in the participant's behavior, character, or relationships that they attribute to the participant's spiritual engagement.
The three-indicator requirement is demanding. It is deliberately so. A stage transition in the Affective Taxonomy is not a mood shift or a momentary resolve. It is a restructuring of the person's value system — the kind of change that Paul describes in Romans 12:2 as a renewing of the mind, and that Jesus describes in the parable of the sower (Matthew 13) as the word taking deep root and bearing fruit. That kind of change leaves multiple traces. Requiring three independent indicators before recording a stage transition protects the protocol against false positives.


**Outcome Variable 2: Scripture Trust Index Score**
The STI-A is administered at baseline, at six months, and at twelve months. A meaningful positive change is defined as movement of one or more points on the overall trust scale, accompanied by qualitative narrative evidence (in the NRP entries) of the specific experiences or encounters with scripture that drove the change. Score change without narrative evidence is recorded but not interpreted as meaningful stage-level progress; the LotS framework predicts that genuine trust development is always associated with specific moments of hearing — specific encounters with the word that cut through in a new way.
The STI-A score also serves as a check on the Affective Taxonomy stage assessment. The two measures should move in the same direction: a person who is progressing on the Affective Taxonomy should show increasing scripture trust, and a person who shows increasing scripture trust should be progressing on the Affective Taxonomy. Where the two measures diverge — a person showing behavioral stage progression but declining scripture trust, for example — the discrepancy is a research flag that invites qualitative investigation. What is happening in this person's relationship to the word that is producing behavioral change without deepening trust?
**Outcome Variable 3: Image of God 4Rs Profile Shift**
The Image of God 4Rs Self-Assessment is administered at baseline and at twelve months. A meaningful shift is defined as a change of two or more points on any single R dimension, or a change in the overall profile that reflects a new balance between the four dimensions. Because the 4Rs are an endpoint description (the shape of the reflection of Christ), they are expected to change more slowly than the Affective Taxonomy stage — a person can be progressing through the early Affective Taxonomy stages without yet showing strong growth in all four Rs. But a person approaching Stage 4 or 5 should show robust growth in at least two Rs, and the specific Rs showing growth should map predictably onto their PROAPT experience: a group that has spent significant time in passages about prayer and relationship with God should show growth in the Relationship R; a group that has wrestled with justice and stewardship passages should show growth in the Rulership R.
**Outcome Variable 4: Integration of Belief and Behavior**
One of the most reliable indicators of Stage 3 formation — and one of the most interesting research variables in the LotS testing program — is the narrowing of the gap between what a person says they believe and what they actually do. A person at Stage 2 can articulate biblical principles with precision while their life is organized around entirely different values. A person at Stage 3 is beginning to feel the cost of that gap, which is why Stage 3 is the stage of personal conviction, repentance, and the first costly behavioral changes. A person at Stage 4 has substantially closed the gap: their life and their beliefs are organized around the same center.
The integration variable is assessed primarily through the External Witness Interview: the person outside the group who knows the participant best is asked, without knowing the participant's self-report scores, to describe the consistency they observe between what the participant says they believe and how they actually live. The EWI question is not asked abstractly — it is grounded in specific observable behaviors: how does the participant handle conflict? How do they talk about money? How do they treat people who cannot help them? What do they do when no one is watching? External witnesses are often the most honest assessors of the belief-behavior gap, because they see the participant in the unguarded moments of daily life that the research group never observes.

## 3d. The Four Assessment Instruments

The individual testing protocol uses four instruments, each targeting a different dimension of the formation journey. Their drafts appear in Appendix A; what follows here is a description of their purpose, their relationship to the LotS framework, and the principles guiding their use.
**Instrument 1: Personal Spiritual Formation Assessment (PSFA)**
The PSFA is a 25-item self-report instrument that assesses the participant's current position on the Affective Taxonomy and their self-perceived growth in the Image of God 4Rs. It is the primary quantitative instrument of the individual testing protocol. The PSFA draws from Hall's (2017) Spiritual Transformation Inventory in its structure — five domains, each with sub-items — while replacing Hall's five domains with the LotS framework's Affective Taxonomy stages (five levels) and adding a second section on the 4Rs (four dimensions, four items each).
The PSFA is administered at baseline, six months, and twelve months. Participants are asked to respond to each item on a five-point scale from "This does not describe me" to "This describes me consistently and habitually." The five-point scale is chosen deliberately to discourage the middle response and to force a direction. The items themselves are written as behavioral and experiential descriptions — not as belief statements ("I believe scripture is true") but as formation statements ("When I read scripture, I find myself making specific changes to my behavior in response"). This is consistent with the Affective Taxonomy's focus on the affective dimension of learning, which is always manifest in behavior and disposition, not just in cognition.
**Instrument 2: Scripture Trust Index — Adapted (STI-A)**
The STI-A is a 12-item self-report instrument adapted from Campbell et al.'s (2021) epistemic trust measure for use in a Christian spiritual formation context. The original instrument measures trust in communicated knowledge in a therapeutic relationship; the LotS adaptation replaces the therapist with scripture as the trusted source and recalibrates the items to reflect the specific dimensions of trust in the biblical text: trust in its reliability, in its personal applicability, in its authority over life decisions, and in its sufficiency as a guide.
The STI-A measures only the trust dimension of Campbell et al.'s three-dimensional construct (trust, mistrust, credulity), because trust is the dimension that maps directly onto the Affective Taxonomy's progression. Credulity — uncritical acceptance of anything claimed in the name of scripture — is not a formation goal; it is a spiritual vulnerability. The STI-A includes two items designed to detect credulity as a possible ceiling effect: a participant who scores very high on trust but also high on credulity items is not at Stage 5 of the Affective Taxonomy; they may be at an immature Stage 2 or 3 where enthusiasm has not yet been tested.
**Instrument 3: Narrative Reflection Protocol (NRP)**
The NRP is the qualitative heart of the individual testing protocol. Each month, the participant responds to four structured reflection prompts in a private journal format. The prompts are designed to surface the specific experiences and encounters that the LotS framework predicts will drive formation: encounters with the word, experiences of testing, moments of heart change, and evidence of Image of God growth.
The four monthly prompts are: (1) Describe a specific moment this month when the word of Christ landed in a new way — when you heard it differently than you had before. What were the circumstances? What changed? (2) Describe a trial or difficulty you encountered this month. In retrospect, how did it affect your trust in God and your engagement with scripture? (3) Describe one specific way your heart changed this month — a shift in what you value, what you desire, what you fear, or what you love. (4) Identify one area where the image of God in you became slightly clearer this month — one of the 4Rs showing a new edge of growth. What made it visible to you?
The NRP entries are not shared with the research team in real time — they are collected at the end of the twelve-month period, with the participant's permission, for qualitative coding. This protects the formation process from becoming a performance: the participant writes for themselves and for God, not for the researcher. At the twelve-month endpoint, the participant reviews their own NRP entries before their final interview, identifying the moments they consider most significant. This review process is itself a formation exercise — a version of the Ignatian Examen applied to a year of documented spiritual journey.
**Instrument 4: Spiritual Disciplines Frequency Log (SDFL)**
The SDFL is a simple weekly log — one page per month — on which the participant records the frequency and approximate duration of five categories of spiritual practice: personal bible reading and study, personal prayer, participation in the PROAPT small group, participation in corporate worship, and acts of service or giving. The SDFL is designed to take less than five minutes per week to complete; if it takes longer, it has been over-designed.
The SDFL is the least theoretically interesting of the four instruments — frequency counts alone tell you almost nothing about formation. But frequency data becomes interpretively rich when correlated with the PSFA stage assessments and the NRP narratives. The SDFL answers the question: under what conditions of practice did this person's formation occur? A participant who shows significant stage progression with low and irregular discipline frequency is an interesting anomaly that invites investigation. A participant whose discipline frequency is high but whose stage progression is flat is pointing toward the most important distinction in the LotS framework: the difference between doing the practices and allowing the word to land in the heart.

## 3e. The Testing Timeline

The twelve-month individual testing protocol is organized into three phases. The phases are not arbitrary divisions; they correspond to the characteristic formation dynamics of the early, middle, and later stages of the Affective Taxonomy journey.
**Phase 1 — Orientation and Baseline (Months 1–3)**
The first three months are foundational. The participant completes the full baseline assessment — PSFA, STI-A, Image of God 4Rs Self-Assessment, and initial SDFL — in the first week. They attend their first PROAPT group sessions and begin to find their footing in the structured bible study rhythm. The facilitator pays close attention during this phase to where each participant is starting: which participants seem to be at Stage 1 (attending, listening, orienting), which are at Stage 2 (engaging intellectually, asking questions), and which are already showing Stage 3 behaviors (applying the word at personal cost).
The NRP begins in Month 1. Many participants will find the early prompts difficult — not because the questions are hard, but because they have never been asked to document their spiritual experience in this specific way. The facilitator should offer encouragement and normalize the awkwardness: the first few months of NRP entries are expected to be the thinnest and most generic, and that itself is data about where the participant is starting.
Phase 1 is also the phase most vulnerable to dropout. Participants who are at Stage 1 — attending out of curiosity or social obligation — may find the commitment level of weekly PROAPT plus monthly NRP higher than they expected. The research protocol should include a check-in conversation at the end of Month 3, giving every participant the opportunity to ask questions, voice concerns, and either recommit or gracefully withdraw before the active formation phase begins.
**Phase 2 — Active Formation (Months 4–9)**
The middle six months are where the work happens. The PROAPT group is now past the initial awkwardness and into a rhythm of genuine engagement with scripture. The NRP entries should begin to show more specificity — particular passages that struck hard, particular trials that shook something loose, particular moments of unexpected heart change. The SDFL data should show whether the participant's discipline patterns are stabilizing or still erratic.
Two formal assessment points fall within Phase 2. At Month 6, the PSFA and STI-A are administered again — not announced as a major assessment event but built into the group's normal rhythm as a natural midpoint check-in. The facilitator reviews each participant's Month 6 scores against their baseline and uses the results to inform their facilitation approach for the second half of the study: participants who are showing clear movement need challenge and deepening; participants who seem stuck need gentle investigation of what may be blocking movement.
The most important formation events of the entire protocol typically occur during Phase 2. The LotS framework predicts, drawing from James 1:2–3 and from the MOVE study's finding about trials as transition catalysts, that the Stage 2–to–3 transition — the movement from intellectual engagement to personal commitment — is almost always associated with a specific challenge or cost. Something happens in Phase 2: a relationship strained, a financial decision made on biblical principle at personal cost, a conviction that requires a public acknowledgment or apology. The NRP is designed to catch these moments. The facilitator's session observation notes are designed to detect when they are approaching.
**Phase 3 — Integration and Assessment (Months 10–12)**
The final three months shift from primarily formative to also evaluative. The participant's NRP entries during this phase should have a different quality than the earlier ones — more reflective, more integrated, more aware of the arc of the year. The SDFL data from these months provides the endpoint picture of the participant's discipline patterns.
At Month 12, the full assessment battery is administered: PSFA, STI-A, and Image of God 4Rs Self-Assessment. The participant reviews their own twelve months of NRP entries before their endpoint interview and identifies the three moments they consider most significant in their formation during the year. The endpoint interview with the research coordinator is semi-structured, following a protocol that allows the participant to tell the story of their year in their own terms before the researcher asks specific questions about stage progression, scripture trust, and Image of God growth.
The External Witness Interview is conducted in Month 12, independent of the participant's own assessment. The external witness is not told what the participant's self-assessment scores are; they are asked only to describe what they have observed in the participant over the past year. The convergence or divergence between the external witness's observations and the participant's self-report is one of the most interesting data points in the entire protocol — and one of the most telling indicators of whether genuine Stage 4 integration has occurred.

## 3f. The Claims This Protocol Tests

The individual testing protocol is designed primarily to test four of the LotS claims identified across Volumes 1–3:
**LotS-H3** — Heart formation is progressive and follows the five stages of the Affective Taxonomy. The protocol tests this by tracking stage-characteristic behaviors over twelve months in a structured formation context. If the claim holds, PROAPT participants should show directional stage progression that is not observed in comparable non-PROAPT participants.
**LotS-H4** — Reflective bible study (PROAPT model) is the primary discipline that advances heart formation at every stage. The protocol tests this through the SDFL frequency data and the NRP narratives, looking for the association between PROAPT engagement and formation movement predicted by the MOVE study (Hawkins & Parkinson, 2011).
**LotS-F2** — Faith is primarily affective, not cognitive. The protocol tests this by examining whether stage progression is associated with changes in the heart dimensions of the PSFA (affect, will, desire) before changes in the cognitive dimensions (knowledge, understanding), as the LotS framework predicts.
**LotS-I2** — Spiritual formation is growth in reflection of the image of God, measurable through the 4Rs. The protocol tests this through the Image of God 4Rs Self-Assessment at baseline and twelve months, looking for profile shifts that correspond to stage progression and to the specific scriptural content engaged through PROAPT.
Secondary claims also touched by this protocol include LotS-F3 (trials as formation mechanism — captured in Phase 2 NRP entries), LotS-H1 (heart as the affective center through which all formation passes — tested by the correlation between PSFA heart-dimension scores and overall stage progression), and LotS-W1 (wisdom development activated by affective desire — tested by the correlation between STI-A trust scores and the emergence of discernment language in later NRP entries).

**Section 3 Discussion Questions**
**1. **The protocol requires a twelve-month commitment to weekly PROAPT and monthly narrative reflection. What would need to be true about a small group or church community for that kind of commitment to be sustainable? What has prevented that level of commitment in your own experience?
**2. **The protocol identifies four baseline dimensions: Affective Taxonomy stage, scripture trust, Image of God profile, and spiritual discipline frequency. If you were to assess your own baseline honestly today, where would you place yourself on each? Which dimension feels most alive, and which most stagnant?
**3. **The External Witness Interview asks someone who knows the participant well to describe what they have observed in them, without knowing the participant's self-report scores. Who in your life would you choose as your external witness? What do you think they would say about the consistency between your stated beliefs and your actual life?
**4. **The protocol predicts that the most significant formation events will occur during the active middle phase — and that they will typically involve a specific trial or cost. Looking back at your own spiritual formation, when was the last time a specific challenge accelerated your growth? What happened? What did it cost?
**5. **The Narrative Reflection Protocol asks four monthly questions: a moment of hearing the word freshly, the impact of a trial, a specific heart change, and evidence of Image of God growth. Try answering all four questions for the current month. What does that exercise reveal about where your formation is most alive right now?
**Section 4: Small Group Spiritual Formation Testing Protocol**
**The Group Is Not Incidental — It Is the Method**
When I think back to what made the original Laws of the Spirit workshop actually work, it was not primarily the questions we were asking, as good as some of them were. It was the four of us in the room together, week after week, refusing to let each other get away with vague spiritual generalities. Tom would push back on a principle with a counter-example from his own experience. Larry would bring a scripture that complicated what we thought we had settled the week before. John would ask the uncomfortable practical question — so what does that mean you are going to do differently on Monday morning? That kind of friction, sustained over time, produced discernment that none of us could have arrived at alone.
The small group is not a delivery mechanism for the Laws of the Spirit. It is the primary testing environment. Individual formation happens in the context of community, is confirmed by community, and is often triggered by the dynamics of community. The PROAPT model's final step — Tell — is not an afterthought; it is the completion of the formation loop. Faith comes from hearing (Romans 10:17), and the small group is where much of that hearing happens: hearing the word, hearing each other's responses to the word, hearing what the Spirit is saying to this particular community in this particular moment.
Section 3 described the Individual Spiritual Formation Testing Protocol — how to track and assess formation in one person over twelve months. Section 4 describes the Small Group Testing Protocol: how to observe and assess the group as a formation unit, how to measure what the group produces that individual formation cannot produce on its own, and how to account for the group's collective journey as a distinct object of study. This section also addresses Open Trail OT-6 — the question of whether a community can be understood to move through the Affective Taxonomy as a collective entity — which was left unresolved in Volume 3.
**4a. Defining the Small Group Testing Unit**
The standard LotS small-group testing unit consists of five to seven participants meeting weekly for 90 minutes using the PROAPT model over a minimum 12-month period. These parameters are not arbitrary. Five to seven is the group size that allows every member to share in every session without the group becoming either a private conversation between two people or a passive audience for one. Ninety minutes is long enough to move through all six PROAPT steps with genuine depth — the Observe and Apply stages in particular require time that shorter formats compress into superficiality. And twelve months is the minimum period over which the Affective Taxonomy stage transitions predicted by the LotS framework are likely to become visible at the group level.
The group should be led by a trained facilitator — someone who understands the Affective Taxonomy, knows the PROAPT model well enough to hold the structure without strangling the Spirit's work within it, and is themselves at Stage 3 or above on the Affective Taxonomy. The facilitator requirement is discussed in more detail in section 4e below, but it is stated here because it is a constraint on the testing unit itself: a group led by a Stage 1 or Stage 2 facilitator is not testing the LotS small group protocol. It is testing an under-resourced approximation of it.
The group composition that best serves the research program is a realistic cross-section of the church population — not a hand-picked group of unusually committed disciples, but a group drawn from the full range of spiritual development stages present in any healthy congregation. Hawkins and Parkinson's (2011) MOVE study found that church populations characteristically cluster at the four growth stages in roughly equal proportions, with fewer people at the most advanced stage. A research group that reflects this distribution — one or two participants at Stage 1, two at Stage 2, two at Stage 3, and one approaching Stage 4 — is both more realistic and more interesting than a homogeneous group, because it allows the protocol to observe how formation dynamics play out across the full range of starting positions simultaneously.
**4b. Open Trail OT-6: Can a Group Move Through the Affective Taxonomy?**
Open Trail OT-6, identified in Volume 3, asks whether a community — a small group, a congregation — can be understood to move through the Affective Taxonomy as a collective entity. The individual Affective Taxonomy is well-grounded: it describes the progressive internalization of a value (in the LotS context, trust in scripture and alignment with the image of God) in a single person's affective life. But can something analogous happen at the group level? Can a group, as a group, move from Receiving to Responding to Valuing to Organization to Characterization?
The working hypothesis proposed here is yes, with two important qualifications. The first qualification is that group-level stage progression is not the same as the average of individual progressions. A group of seven people in which four have moved from Stage 2 to Stage 3 over twelve months has not simply achieved a higher average score. Something has happened to the group's culture, its shared language, its collective willingness to engage the word at personal cost. The quality of the group's life together has changed. That change is observable and meaningful, and it is not fully captured by aggregating individual scores.
The second qualification is that the group's collective stage is disproportionately influenced by two factors: the facilitator's stage and the group's most advanced members. This is consistent with what we know about community formation dynamics from both social psychology and scripture: groups tend to be pulled upward toward their most formation-mature members, and facilitators set the ceiling for the depth of vulnerability and application that the group will risk. A Stage 4 facilitator creates a group culture in which Stage 3 behavior is normalized and Stage 4 behavior is modeled. A Stage 2 facilitator creates a group culture in which intellectual engagement is the ceiling.


**4c. The Group Spiritual Formation Profile (GSFP)**
The Group Spiritual Formation Profile is a visual mapping tool that shows the distribution of individual Affective Taxonomy stages within the group at any given assessment point. It is updated quarterly by the facilitator, based on their Stage assessment of each participant, and serves as the primary instrument for tracking the group's collective development over the twelve-month period.
The GSFP is not a ranking of group members — it is never shared with the group in a way that identifies individuals by stage. Its purpose is to give the facilitator and research coordinator a picture of the group's composition and how it is evolving. A GSFP that shows, at Month 3, three participants at Stage 2 and two at Stage 3, and that shows, at Month 12, those same two at Stage 3 and three at Stage 3 with two approaching Stage 4, is telling a story about collective formation that no single participant's individual arc could tell alone.


The example GSFP above illustrates the kind of movement the LotS protocol predicts: a net upward shift across the group, with the most advanced participants continuing to grow and the least advanced participants moving into genuine engagement (Stage 2 to Stage 3 transition) over the course of the year. It also illustrates a realistic complexity: not every participant moves at the same rate, and at Month 12, the group still has one participant at Stage 2. That participant's presence in the group is not a failure of the protocol. A Stage 2 participant embedded in a Stage 3 group culture is exposed, week after week, to the normalized behaviors of the next stage, which is exactly the formation environment the LotS framework predicts will eventually catalyze their own transition.
**INSTRUMENT CROSS-REFERENCE — NCD’s 3 Colors of Community: **A specific external validity check is worth naming here as a concrete action item for the multi-site research design. Schwarz’s The 3 Colors of Community (ChurchSmart, 2012) includes an empirically-developed communal test instrument that assesses small group health across seven communal qualities — mapped, in Schwarz’s framework, against the seven deadly sins as their corresponding blockage counterparts. The overlap with Vol 2’s emotional knots framework is not incidental: where Vol 2 identifies the ways individual affective distortions obstruct formation, Schwarz’s seven communal qualities identify the collective-level analogs — the patterns of group dysfunction that prevent a community from becoming the formation environment it is meant to be.
Administering both the GSFP and the NCD 3 Colors of Community communal instrument to the same groups in any multi-site study would allow the research team to assess convergent validity between the two instruments. If a group that the GSFP identifies as formation-blocked also scores low on the relevant NCD communal quality, that convergence would provide evidence that the two instruments are measuring related constructs from different angles. If they diverge, that divergence is equally informative — it would indicate that the GSFP is capturing something the NCD instrument misses, or vice versa. Either result advances the measurement program. This is a low-cost addition to the research design and a high-return one.
**4d. Group-Level Outcome Variables**
The small group testing protocol tracks five group-level outcome variables over the twelve-month period. These are distinct from the individual outcome variables described in Section 3, though they are correlated with them. They describe what is happening to the group as a unit — its shared culture, its collective behaviors, and its effectiveness as a formation community.
**Group Outcome Variable 1: Net Stage Progression Across the Group**
The primary group-level outcome variable is the net upward movement of Affective Taxonomy stages across all participants over the twelve-month period. This is calculated as the sum of all positive stage movements across the group, minus any negative movements (stage regression, which can occur during periods of significant trial or spiritual crisis). A group with seven participants, in which five show positive stage movement of at least one stage transition each, is performing strongly on this variable.
This variable directly tests LotS-H3 and LotS-H4 at the group level: if reflective bible study in community produces the progressive heart formation the Affective Taxonomy describes, then PROAPT groups should show meaningfully greater net stage progression than comparable groups not engaged in structured reflective bible study. The multi-site research design proposed in Section 5 is built around this comparison.
**Group Outcome Variable 2: Group Cohesion and Psychological Safety**
Stage 3 formation — the transition from intellectual engagement to personal commitment — requires a specific quality of group environment: enough trust and safety for a participant to share a personal conviction, acknowledge a sin, or report a costly behavioral change without fear of judgment or embarrassment. A group that remains at a surface level of politeness — where no one ever takes a real personal risk — is not a Stage 3 group, regardless of what the participants' individual PSFA scores say.
Group cohesion is assessed quarterly using an adapted Group Cohesion Scale (GCS), a brief validated instrument drawn from the group therapy literature and modified for a spiritual formation context. The GCS asks participants to rate the degree to which they feel genuinely known by the group, safe to share honestly, and supported in their formation journey. A rising GCS score over the twelve-month period is both an outcome of the formation process and a precondition for the deeper stages of formation — the two are mutually reinforcing.
The cohesion variable also functions as an early warning system. A group whose GCS scores are declining or flat at Month 6 is unlikely to produce Stage 3-level formation in its remaining six months without a significant intervention. The facilitator's observation notes and the Session Observation Protocol data from this period should be examined carefully to identify what is preventing the development of the psychological safety that Stage 3 formation requires.
**Group Outcome Variable 3: Epistemic Trust in the Group Process**
The LotS framework proposes, drawing from Campbell et al.'s (2021) epistemic trust model, that the group itself becomes a trusted source of communal discernment over time — not just a context in which individuals hear scripture, but a community whose collective reading of scripture and life is trusted as a source of formation guidance. This is the communal dimension of the Romans 10 chain: sending, preaching, hearing, believing — and the hearing happens, in part, through the voices of trusted community members speaking the word into specific life situations.
Epistemic trust in the group process is assessed through two items added to the quarterly GCS: one item asking whether the participant trusts the group's collective discernment as a source of guidance for their own life decisions, and one item asking whether they have acted on something the group discerned together in the past quarter. Movement on these two items over twelve months is a group-level indicator of the developing communal trust that the LotS framework predicts will emerge in a maturing PROAPT group.
**Group Outcome Variable 4: The Tell Rate**
The PROAPT model's final step — Tell — asks every participant to name who they will tell about what they heard from the word this week, and to report back the following week on whether they told them. The Tell step is both a formation practice and a research observable: it is the point at which the formation that happened in the group room crosses the threshold into the participant's ordinary life and begins to operate in the Romans 10 chain.
The Tell Rate is defined as the percentage of participants, across all sessions, who complete the Tell step — both naming a person and reporting back on the conversation. A group with a high Tell Rate is a group in which the word is being carried outward from the formation community into the wider world. A group with a low Tell Rate is a group in which the formation is staying in the room. The Tell Rate is tracked by the facilitator in the Session Observation Protocol and reported monthly.
The Tell Rate is also sensitive to the group stage. At Stage 2, participants are often reluctant to Tell — sharing their scripture engagement with someone outside the group feels vulnerable and potentially awkward. At Stage 3, participants begin to Tell naturally, because they are beginning to live from the word rather than just engaging with it intellectually. At Stage 4 and 5, Tell is not a prompted exercise but a habit: these participants are continuously, without being asked, bringing the word to bear on the conversations of their daily lives. Rising Tell Rates over the twelve-month period are therefore a reasonable proxy for group-level stage progression.
**Group Outcome Variable 5: Fruit Observed by External Witnesses**
The final group-level outcome variable is the most demanding and the most interesting: evidence of the fruit of the Spirit (Galatians 5:22–23) visible to people outside the group. Love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, self-control — these are Stage 4 and Stage 5 indicators, and they are most reliably observed not by the person exhibiting them (who is often the last to notice) and not by the formation group (who may be too close to the person to see clearly), but by the people in the participant's ordinary life who have no stake in the research program and no reason to be generous in their assessment.
The External Witness Interviews described in Section 3 aggregate across the group to produce a group-level picture. If, at Month 12, six of seven participants' external witnesses independently report observable character changes that they attribute to the participant's deepened spiritual engagement, that is a strong group-level indicator that the formation process is producing the outcomes the LotS framework predicts. If the external witnesses report that nothing has changed — or, more troublingly, that the participant has become more self-righteous or less relationally available since joining the group — that is a finding that demands honest examination.
**4e. The Facilitator as a Critical Variable**
I want to be direct about something that research designs often soften: the facilitator is probably the single most important variable in whether a PROAPT small group produces genuine formation. Not the curriculum, not the assessment instruments, not the group composition — the person in the room who holds the space, models what engagement with the word looks like from the inside, and creates the conditions in which the Stage 2-to-3 transition becomes possible.
This is consistent with the Image of God framework from Volume 3. The facilitator is not just a discussion leader. They are an under-shepherd of God's kingdom in this particular formation context — responsible, as Kilner's Rulership dimension describes, to steward the spiritual growth of the people in their care in alignment with God's purposes. A facilitator who is themselves at Stage 2 of the Affective Taxonomy — for whom scripture is interesting but not yet personally binding — cannot facilitate Stage 3 formation in others, because they cannot model what it looks like to let the word cost something.
**Facilitator Formation Requirements**
The LotS testing protocol requires that all group facilitators meet three criteria before leading a research group. First, they must be assessed at Stage 3 or above on the Affective Taxonomy, confirmed by both self-report and an observer who knows them well. A Stage 3 facilitator can lead a group that includes Stage 4 participants — the Spirit's work is not limited by the facilitator's stage — but a facilitator below Stage 3 cannot reliably model the behaviors that the protocol is trying to produce.
Second, they must have completed a minimum of six months of their own PROAPT practice before leading a research group. PROAPT facilitation requires a kind of dual attention — to the scripture text, to the group's dynamics, to the Spirit's movement in the room, to the individual needs of each participant — that only develops through sustained practice. A facilitator who is learning PROAPT at the same time as they are leading a research group is not testing the LotS protocol; they are running a pilot of an undertrained facilitator.
Third, they must be receiving supervision from a more formation-mature practitioner throughout the research period — a spiritual director, a senior pastor, or a peer supervision group of facilitators who meet monthly to share observations and receive challenge and support. The supervision requirement is both a methodological safeguard (it catches facilitator drift and blind spots before they affect the group's formation) and a formation requirement: the facilitator's own journey does not pause while they are leading others.
**The Facilitator's Own Affective Taxonomy Stage as an Independent Variable**
The research protocol treats the facilitator's Affective Taxonomy stage as a formal independent variable. At the beginning of the study, each facilitator is assessed using the PSFA and the observer-report process described in Section 3. Their stage is documented and held constant as a variable in the analysis of group-level outcomes. The prediction — and this is an Open Trail worth testing formally — is that groups led by Stage 4 facilitators will show significantly greater net stage progression over twelve months than groups led by Stage 3 facilitators, even when all other variables (group composition, church context, PROAPT fidelity) are controlled.
This prediction is not a criticism of Stage 3 facilitators. Stage 3 is a genuinely transformative stage of formation, and a Stage 3 facilitator brings real gifts to a group — the lived experience of what it costs to begin organizing life around biblical values, the fresh memory of the Stage 2-to-3 transition that may be happening for participants around them. But Stage 4 brings something different: the stability and integration that comes from having organized the entire value system around scripture, from which a different quality of presence and patience is possible. The distinction is worth testing, not assuming.
**4f. The Session Observation Protocol**
The Session Observation Protocol (SOP) is a structured one-page checklist that the facilitator completes after every PROAPT session. It takes five to ten minutes and is designed to capture the formation-relevant dynamics of the session in a form that can be aggregated across sessions and analyzed over time.
The SOP tracks six session-level variables: (1) the number of participants who engaged substantively in the Observe step — asking factual questions, making connections between the text and prior sessions; (2) the number who shared personal applications in the Apply step that reflected genuine personal cost or conviction; (3) the number who reported a Tell from the previous week; (4) the number who named a specific person for this week's Tell; (5) any significant emotional moment in the session — a moment of visible conviction, repentance, or breakthrough that the facilitator identifies as a potential stage transition indicator; and (6) the facilitator's overall rating of the session's depth of formation engagement on a five-point scale.
The SOP data builds into a session-by-session formation record for each group. Plotted over twelve months, the SOP scores tell a story: groups that begin with low Apply and Tell scores and gradually develop richer personal engagement are tracking the Stage 2-to-3 movement that the LotS protocol predicts. Groups whose SOP scores are flat or declining after Month 3 are signaling that something in the group's formation environment is not working, and the qualitative data from the facilitator's supervision notes should be examined to understand what.
**4g. The Collective Spiritual Formation Assessment**
In addition to the individual PSFA scores and the facilitator's GSFP tracking, the testing protocol includes a brief Collective Spiritual Formation Assessment (CSFA) administered to the group as a whole at the three formal assessment points: baseline, Month 6, and Month 12. The CSFA asks participants to evaluate the group itself — not their own formation, but what they observe in the group's shared culture and collective life with scripture.
The CSFA has twelve items organized into three domains. The first domain assesses the group's corporate engagement with scripture: do participants experience the group's reading and discussion of the word as qualitatively different from reading alone? Do they find that the group hears things they would not have heard individually? The second domain assesses the group's corporate trust and safety: do participants feel genuinely known, genuinely safe to share their formation journey, and genuinely accountable to the group's collective discernment? The third domain assesses the group's outward orientation: is the formation that happens in the room crossing the threshold into the participants' daily lives, relationships, and communities?
The CSFA is administered anonymously, and results are shared with the group in aggregate at the Month 6 and Month 12 check-in sessions. This shared feedback is itself a formation practice: a group that can look honestly at its own CSFA scores — noting where its corporate life is strong and where it is thin — and discuss those results with humility and curiosity, is exhibiting Stage 3 behaviors at the group level. A group that receives CSFA feedback defensively, or that cannot discuss its collective weaknesses without becoming conflictual, is showing the facilitator exactly what formation work remains to be done.
**4h. The Testing Timeline for Small Groups**
The small-group testing timeline mirrors the individual timeline's three-phase structure while adding group-level assessment events and a layer of facilitator-side data collection that has no individual parallel.
**Phase 1 — Group Formation and Baseline (Months 1–3)**
The group's first three months are foundational in a double sense: both the individual participants are establishing their formation baselines (as described in Section 3), and the group itself is forming as a community. These two processes interact. The quality of the group's formation as a community — the degree to which psychological safety develops, the degree to which the Tell step becomes normal, the degree to which participants begin to trust the group's discernment — is both a precondition for and a product of the individual formation happening within it.
The facilitator's primary task in Phase 1 is to model PROAPT discipline without over-controlling the group's responses, to establish the norms of honest sharing and genuine application that will define the group's culture for the next twelve months, and to notice — with as much accuracy as possible — where each participant is starting. The GSFP is first populated at the end of Month 1, updated at the end of Month 3.
The CSFA baseline is administered at the end of Month 1, before the group has had time to develop much shared history. This early baseline captures each participant's initial experience of the group and serves as the reference point for measuring the group's collective development. Groups whose Month 1 CSFA scores already show high trust and safety have likely been formed by prior shared history — the participants may know each other well from other church contexts. This is a variable to document, because it affects the trajectory of the group's formation differently than a group of relative strangers finding their way toward trust for the first time.
**Phase 2 — Group Deepening (Months 4–9)**
By Month 4, the group should have moved past the initial politeness and orientation of Phase 1 into a rhythm of genuine engagement. The SOP data from Phase 2 is the richest seam of formation evidence in the entire protocol: session by session, the facilitator is documenting the specific moments of Apply and Tell, the specific emotional breakthroughs, the specific instances of personal risk-taking with the word.
The Month 6 assessment point is a significant event for both the group and individuals. The GSFP is updated, the individual PSFAs and STI-As are administered, and the CSFA is administered to the group. The facilitator then prepares a brief Group Formation Review — a one-to-two-page narrative summary of the group's journey to date, identifying the most significant formation moments, the current Group Affective Taxonomy Stage, and the primary formation opportunities and obstacles for the remaining six months. This review is shared with the research coordinator and forms the basis for the facilitator's supervision conversation at Month 6.
Open Trail OT-5 — the question of what precedes instantaneous or dramatic heart change — is most likely to yield data during Phase 2. If any participant in the group experiences a significant formation breakthrough during this period (a dramatic conviction, a sudden reorganization of values, an unexpected surge of Stage 3 or Stage 4 behavior), the protocol calls for a brief supplementary interview with that participant within two weeks of the event, documenting the conditions that preceded it: what had been happening in their PROAPT engagement, their personal circumstances, their prayer life, their relational context. This interview data is not part of the primary research protocol; it is flagged as supplementary qualitative data for the OT-5 Open Trail.
**Phase 3 — Group Integration and Assessment (Months 10–12)**
The final three months of the group's testing period have a different quality than the preceding nine. By Month 10, the group has a shared history: shared passages that have marked them, shared moments of vulnerability and breakthrough, shared language for describing the formation journey. The PROAPT sessions during this phase should be the deepest of the year — not because the group is trying harder, but because Stage 3 and Stage 4 formation behaviors have become habitual and the word is landing on ground that has been progressively prepared over nine months of consistent engagement.
The Month 12 endpoint assessment completes all individual instruments (PSFA, STI-A, Image of God 4Rs) and adds the CSFA endpoint administration and the facilitator's final GSFP update. The facilitator also completes a Final Group Formation Report — a narrative document of three to five pages describing the group's twelve-month journey, the key formation events and transitions observed, the current Group Affective Taxonomy Stage, and the facilitator's assessment of which aspects of the PROAPT protocol were most and least effective in producing the outcomes the LotS framework predicted.
The External Witness Interviews are conducted during Months 11 and 12. The research coordinator aggregates the EWI data across the group and presents the group-level summary (without individual identification) to the facilitator and, at the group's discretion, to the group itself. The aggregate EWI data — what the people who know these participants best observed in them over the year — is the most honest mirror the group will encounter of whether its formation produced the fruit it was aiming for.
**4i. The Claims This Protocol Tests**
The small group testing protocol tests several LotS claims that the individual protocol cannot reach on its own, as well as providing a community-level confirmation of the claims tested individually.
**LotS-F4** — Community is essential to faith formation (the Romans 10 chain). The group protocol tests this directly: does PROAPT community produce formation outcomes that cannot be attributed to the individual's own discipline practice alone? The comparison between individual SDFL discipline frequency and group-level SOP Tell Rate scores should show that the group's communal engagement with the word adds something above and beyond what any participant would produce in private.
**LotS-H2** — Unrepentant sin progressively hardens the heart. The group protocol tests this as a qualitative theme: do the NRP narratives and EWI interviews reveal patterns in which participants who are carrying unresolved sin show consistently slower stage progression? This is a sensitive and ethically demanding area of investigation, and the protocol handles it through aggregate qualitative coding rather than individual attribution.
**OT-6 (Collective formation)** — The group protocol is specifically designed to test whether the Group Affective Taxonomy Stage construct is meaningful and useful: does it predict individual formation outcomes better than individual starting stages alone? Does the facilitator stage predict group GATS trajectory? Does the CSFA's corporate engagement domain correlate with the Tell Rate and net stage progression?
**LotS-SF1 and LotS-SF2** — Spiritual force and energy. The group protocol contributes to these claims through the aggregate EWI data and the facilitator's Final Group Formation Report: do groups at higher average stages show qualitatively different levels of spiritual vitality — more Tell activity, more fruit observed by external witnesses, more evidence of the word operating in the participants' daily lives — than groups at lower stages? The spiritual force claims are the hardest in the LotS framework to operationalize, and the group protocol is where the most interesting qualitative data about them is likely to emerge.

**Section 5: Research Program Design for Spiritual Formation**
**From Protocol to Program**
Sections 3 and 4 described what to measure and how to measure it — in an individual over twelve months, and in a small group over the same period. Section 5 asks the harder question: how do you build a research program that can say something meaningful, not just about this group in this church in this season, but about the Laws of the Spirit as a set of claims that hold — or do not hold — across persons, contexts, and time?
The step from protocol to program is not just a scaling question. It introduces methodological demands that the individual and group protocols alone cannot satisfy: the need for comparison conditions, the need for sufficient sample sizes to support statistical analysis, the need for site diversity that allows claims to be distinguished from local church culture effects, and the need for longitudinal depth that allows the slow work of Affective Taxonomy stage progression to become visible against the noise of ordinary life variation. These demands are real, and this section takes them seriously.
At the same time, I want to hold onto something that can easily get lost when a personal exploration enters the academy: the research program proposed here is not an end in itself. It is a service to the original question — the one that started with a Saturday morning workshop group in 2002 and has been working its way through other groups I have been involved in using scripture, reason, experience, and community ever since. Does the spiritual world operate according to regular, discoverable laws? The research program is one more attempt to find out — the most rigorous attempt the LotS project has yet made. But the question it is trying to answer is the same one it has always been.
**5a. The Five Primary Research Questions**
The research program is organized around five primary research questions. Each question is testable with the instruments and protocols described in Sections 3 and 4; each maps to one or more specific LotS claims; and each is designed to yield findings that are meaningful whether they confirm or challenge the framework.






**5b. Research Design**
The LotS research program employs a longitudinal mixed-methods design with a quasi-experimental comparison condition. The choice of design reflects the epistemological framework established in Section 2: the complexity of spiritual formation outcomes requires both quantitative tracking of behavioral change and qualitative depth in understanding what that change means and how it occurred. Neither alone is adequate; together they provide the multi-layered picture of formation that the LotS claims require.
**Overall Design Structure**
The program runs for a minimum of twenty-four months across three phases: a six-month preparation and recruitment phase, a twelve-month active study period (the individual and group protocols described in Sections 3 and 4), and a six-month analysis and reporting phase. The extended preparation phase is not optional padding — it is the time required to recruit and train facilitators, establish research partnerships with participating churches, adapt and pilot-test the assessment instruments, and ensure that the ethical framework described in section 5d is in place before any participant data is collected.
The active study period is the twelve months described in Sections 3 and 4. During this period, data collection happens through the instruments already described: PSFA, STI-A, Image of God 4Rs, SDFL, NRP, SOP, GCS, CSFA, and EWI. No new data collection instruments are introduced mid-study; this is a constraint that protects the study's integrity and reduces participant burden.
**Sites and Groups**
The research program targets three to five participating churches across different demographic and theological contexts. Site diversity is essential for the LotS program's ambition to say something about the Laws of the Spirit that transcends any single church culture. A finding that holds at a theologically conservative suburban congregation, a mid-size urban multiethnic congregation, and a smaller rural church is meaningfully more robust than a finding from a single site, however well-designed that site's study is.
The aspiration to multi-site, eventually international research is not unprecedented in Christian spiritual formation research, and naming the closest existing model is useful both for credibility and for realistic planning. Christian Schwarz’s original NCD study — published in 1996 as the empirical foundation of Natural Church Development — began in German-speaking Europe with 120 churches, published the findings, and built from there through a staged growth model: local pilot, then multi-site domestic study, then international partnership. By 2016, the NCD Church Survey had been administered to more than 70,000 churches across 84 countries and 112 denominations. Schwarz did not design a 70,000-church study. He designed a 120-church study rigorously, published results that warranted replication, and let the research program grow into its international scale through the credibility of its findings.
The LotS research trajectory should follow the same logic. The three-to-five site design proposed here is not a scaled-down version of a larger ambition that has been deferred for lack of resources. It is the right first study — the study that, if it produces the results the LotS framework predicts, will provide the warrant for the next stage. A finding that PROAPT groups show meaningfully greater stage progression than comparison cohorts across three to five theologically and demographically diverse sites is the result that invites replication. That invitation, if answered, is how a 120-church study becomes a 70,000-church study over twenty years. The LotS program should be designed from the beginning with that trajectory in mind.
Each site runs two PROAPT research groups simultaneously: one led by a Stage 3 facilitator and one by a Stage 4 facilitator, with comparable group compositions at baseline. This within-site pairing controls for site-level variables (church culture, demographic factors, theological tradition) while testing RQ4 — the facilitator stage question — within the same context. The research coordinator visits each site at least quarterly to conduct instrument administrations, facilitator supervision check-ins, and spot-observation of PROAPT sessions.
Each site also identifies a comparison cohort: church members who are not participating in a PROAPT research group but who agree to complete the PSFA and STI-A at baseline and at Month 12. The comparison cohort is not randomly assigned — it is a quasi-experimental control, drawn from the same church population. Participants in the comparison cohort may be attending other small groups, Sunday school classes, or no formal formation program. Their twelve-month data provides the baseline rate of stage progression in the church population without the PROAPT intervention, which is the denominator against which the PROAPT groups' progression is measured for RQ1.


**Quantitative Analysis Plan**
The quantitative analysis addresses RQ1 through RQ4 using the PSFA stage scores, STI-A scores, and Image of God 4Rs assessment scores collected at three time points (baseline, Month 6, Month 12) across PROAPT participants and the comparison cohort.
For RQ1, the primary analysis is a repeated-measures comparison of PSFA stage progression between the PROAPT groups and the comparison cohort over twelve months. Statistical significance is tested using a mixed ANOVA with time as the within-subjects factor and group condition (PROAPT vs. comparison) as the between-subjects factor. The expected finding is a significant time × condition interaction: PROAPT participants show greater stage progression over time than comparison participants. Effect size will be reported as partial eta-squared; a medium effect (η² ≥ 0.06) is the minimum threshold for the finding to be considered practically meaningful given the sample size.
For RQ2, the analysis is a correlation study at each time point: are PSFA stage scores and STI-A trust scores significantly correlated? A Pearson r ≥ 0.60 is predicted, based on the theoretical relationship between the two constructs. A second analysis examines the temporal ordering of the two measures: does affective stage progression (PSFA) precede scripture trust changes (STI-A) or follow them? This temporal ordering question is addressed using cross-lagged panel analysis across the three time points.
For RQ3, the analysis examines the correlation between PSFA stage progression and Image of God 4Rs profile shifts. A separate analysis examines whether specific 4Rs grow in response to specific scriptural content domains — this requires the facilitator's session notes to document the primary thematic content of each month's PROAPT sessions, allowing the research coordinator to code sessions by 4R domain and test whether 4R growth aligns with content exposure.
For RQ4, the analysis compares net stage progression across groups led by Stage 3 versus Stage 4 facilitators, using a two-sample t-test on net PSFA change scores within each site. Given the small number of groups (two per site, ten total), the analysis will be primarily descriptive and effect-size-based rather than inferential — the sample size is not sufficient to support strong statistical conclusions about the facilitator effect, but a consistent directional pattern across sites will be a meaningful finding that warrants further investigation in a larger study.
**Qualitative Analysis Plan**
The qualitative analysis addresses RQ5 and provides the interpretive texture for RQ1 through RQ4. Its primary data sources are the twelve months of NRP entries from each participant, the facilitator session observation notes, the OT-5 supplementary interview transcripts, and the EWI interview data.
The NRP entries are analyzed using thematic coding organized around the LotS framework's key constructs: hearing the word (Romans 10:17 mechanism), trial as formation catalyst (James 1:2–3), heart change (Affective Taxonomy stage transitions), and Image of God growth (4R domains). Two coders work independently on a random 30 percent sample of the NRP entries to establish inter-rater reliability before coding the full dataset. Discrepancies are resolved through discussion and a third-coder tiebreak process. The coding scheme is developed inductively from the data as well as deductively from the LotS framework — findings that do not map onto the existing framework are flagged as potential refinements or challenges to the framework, not discarded.
The EWI data is analyzed separately, with coders working blind to the participant's own PSFA and NRP data. This blind analysis is essential: the value of the EWI is precisely its independence from the participant's self-report. Convergence between the EWI's observations and the participant's own NRP narrative is a validity indicator for both instruments. Divergence — particularly where the participant reports significant heart change that the external witness does not observe — is a flag for what the LotS framework calls the belief-behavior integration gap: Stage 3 formation at the level of intention that has not yet fully crossed into Stage 4 at the level of habitual character.
The OT-5 supplementary interviews with participants who experienced rapid or dramatic stage transitions are analyzed using narrative inquiry methods: each participant tells the story of their formation breakthrough in their own words, and the researcher maps that narrative against the LotS framework's predictions about preconditions. Consistent patterns across multiple supplementary interviews would constitute preliminary evidence for the OT-5 hypothesis — that instantaneous-seeming heart change is characteristically preceded by a period of spiritual preparation that may not be visible to the participant at the time.

**5d. Ethical Considerations**
Research in spiritual formation settings raises ethical questions that standard social science research frameworks address imperfectly. The participants are not just research subjects — they are people entrusting their spiritual journey, their personal convictions, their private struggles with faith, to a process that they are simultaneously trying to live authentically. The researcher is not just an observer — they are, in the LotS framework, themselves a disciple, bringing their own formation history and their own stage on the Affective Taxonomy to every interaction with the data. These realities demand an ethical framework that is more thoughtful than the standard IRB checklist, though that checklist is the minimum, not the ceiling.
**Informed Consent in a Pastoral Context**
Every participant in the research program — PROAPT group members, comparison cohort members, external witnesses, and facilitators — signs an informed consent document before any data is collected. The consent document describes the research purpose, the data collection methods, the storage and anonymization procedures, the right to withdraw at any time without affecting their participation in the church's regular programming, and the potential risks of participation.
The potential risks in a spiritual formation study are not primarily physical — they are relational and psychological. A participant who shares a personal conviction or a pattern of unrepentant sin in the context of the PROAPT group, and who later regrets that disclosure, cannot fully un-share it. A participant whose EWI reveals a significant belief-behavior gap may find the endpoint interview conversation uncomfortable or even painful. A participant who grows in internalization may cause external relationships (such as a spouse) outside the research group uncomfortable and cause the participant to suppress development. The consent document names these risks explicitly and describes the support available: the facilitator is trained to respond pastorally to distress that arises in the group context, and the research coordinator maintains a referral list of pastoral counselors at each site.
The consent process for external witnesses deserves special attention. External witnesses are asked to describe, in an interview, what they have observed in the participant over twelve months. They may not know that the participant is in a spiritual formation research program; they may be surprised by the depth of some of the interview questions. The EWI consent process includes a brief explanation of the research purpose and an explicit statement that the interview data will be anonymized before any analysis and will never be shared with the participant in a form that identifies the external witness by name.
**Protecting Vulnerable Participants**
Three categories of participant require particular care in the LotS research program.
**Participants in early stages of faith (Stage 1–2).** The assessment process — particularly the PSFA's direct questions about one's relationship to scripture and the degree to which biblical principles are shaping daily life — can feel exposing or judgmental to a participant who is genuinely at Stage 1: attending out of curiosity, not yet sure what they believe, not yet trusting the group enough to share honestly. The PSFA is framed as a personal reflection tool, not an evaluation. Facilitators are trained to reinforce this framing and to respond to Stage 1 participants' discomfort with pastoral warmth rather than research urgency.
**Participants in spiritual crisis.** The formation process can surface pain, loss, unresolved grief, and deep spiritual doubt. A participant who is in acute spiritual crisis — questioning whether God exists, processing a devastating loss, or wrestling with a long-buried sin — is not best served by continued participation in a research protocol. The facilitator protocol includes clear criteria for recognizing acute spiritual crisis and clear procedures for responding: stepping outside the research protocol to offer pastoral care, referring to professional support when appropriate, and supporting the participant's decision to withdraw from the research program without prejudice if that is what they need.
**Participants who may feel assessed or judged.** The entire vocabulary of the Affective Taxonomy — stages, transitions, progression — can inadvertently communicate that some participants are "better" than others, more spiritually advanced, further along. This is the opposite of the LotS project's intention, and it is the opposite of the Image of God framework's anthropology: every participant is a fully connected image-bearer, whatever stage they are at. The facilitator training program addresses this directly, giving facilitators language for speaking about stage progression in ways that honor the dignity and the particular journey of every participant, at every stage.
**The Researcher's Own Formation as an Ethical Commitment**
The LotS project has always insisted, from the first workshop group onward, that you cannot study the spiritual life from the outside. This insistence has an ethical dimension that deserves to be named explicitly in the research framework. A researcher who is not themselves engaged in the formation journey — who is observing without practicing, measuring without living — is asking participants to be transparent about their spiritual struggles while maintaining their own opacity. That is not an ethical research relationship.
The research program therefore requires that all research coordinators be themselves engaged in a regular formation practice — minimally, personal reflective bible study and participation in a small group — and that they maintain a personal journal of their own formation observations during the study period. This journal is not analyzed as research data; it is the researcher's own formation record, maintained as an act of integrity and a calibration instrument for their interpretive work with the participant data. The most natural small group of the coordinators is the other involved coordinators, if possible.
This requirement will strike some academic researchers as methodologically unusual. It is. It reflects the LotS project's foundational conviction that the spiritual world is not a subject to be studied from a safe distance but a reality to be inhabited — that the Laws of the Spirit, if they are real, are no more observable from the outside than the law of love is observable by someone who has never loved. The research program is built for practitioners who are also rigorous thinkers. That is a narrow target. It is the right one.

**5f. What a Positive Finding Would Mean — and What It Would Not**
I want to close Section 5 with the question that, in my experience, almost never gets asked but should be asked first: if this research program produces positive findings — if PROAPT groups show significantly greater stage progression than comparison participants, if scripture trust and stage progression correlate as predicted, if facilitator stage predicts group outcomes — what would that actually mean?
It would mean that the mechanisms proposed in Volumes 1–3 — reflective bible study in community, progressive heart formation through the Affective Taxonomy stages, growth toward the Image of God through the 4Rs — produce measurable, reproducible outcomes in real people's lives over real time. It would mean that the LotS framework is not just a personal exploration or a theological speculation but a testable model of how the spiritual life works, with enough empirical traction to be refined, replicated, and built upon. It would mean that the original workshop group in 2002 was onto something real.
It would not mean that the Laws of the Spirit are exhaustively described in this three-volume series. The LotS project has always been an open exploration, not a closed system. A positive finding from the research program would be an invitation to go deeper, not a declaration that the work is done. It would not mean that the Holy Spirit's work can be reduced to measurable outcomes — the research program captures the traceable shadow of spiritual formation, not its full reality. And it would not mean that any individual who follows the PROAPT model and engages sincerely with the Affective Taxonomy framework is guaranteed specific outcomes on a specific timeline. The Spirit blows where He wishes. The research program is testing whether the conditions proposed by the LotS framework are conditions in which the Spirit characteristically works — not whether they compel Him.
What a negative finding would mean is equally worth stating. If PROAPT groups show no greater stage progression than comparison participants — if the Tell Rate stays flat, if the EWI data shows no observable change in participants' character and behavior, if the NRP narratives are thin and generic after twelve months of weekly engagement — that would be significant disconfirming evidence for LotS-H4, and it would demand a serious re-examination of the framework. Not its abandonment — a single study is not dispositive — but a serious, honest, community-tested re-examination of whether the proposed mechanisms are the right ones.
That is the kind of project the LotS has always been. The test is not just for the data. It is for us.
