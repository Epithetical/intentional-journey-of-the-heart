**Preface**
I want to be clear about what this volume is and what it is not. Volumes 1, 2, and 3 were explorations — the record of a journey that started in 2002 with a small workshop group at our church and grew, slowly and sometimes painfully, into a set of claims about how the spiritual world works. Those volumes are my best current answer to the question I have been asking since I came to Christ in my thirties: if God created a world that runs on regular, discoverable laws, then shouldn't His spiritual world run on regular, discoverable laws too?
Volume 4 is different. Volume 4 asks the harder question: how would we know?
That question is uncomfortable in church circles. We are not supposed to test God (Deuteronomy 6:16). But there is a difference between testing God — demanding that He perform on our terms — and testing a framework for understanding how He works, to see whether the framework actually holds up in real people's lives over real time. The second kind of testing is not only permitted; it is commanded. "Test everything; hold fast what is good" (1 Thessalonians 5:21). "Dear friends, do not believe every spirit, but test the spirits to see whether they are from God" (1 John 4:1).
C.S. Lewis adds a warning that belongs here, before the methodology is built. In The Screwtape Letters he notes that the enemy’s most effective counterfeits are not obvious falsehoods but near-truths — experiences and impressions close enough to the genuine article that only a well-formed discernment can tell the difference. His implication is that the tester matters as much as the test: a person of immature character applying a rigorous methodology may still be reliably deceived, because the deception is calibrated to what rigorous but immature discernment will miss. This does not undermine the testing project — it contextualizes it. The protocols in the sections that follow are necessary; they are not sufficient. The researcher’s own formation — their fear of the Lord, their community accountability, their willingness to be wrong — is not a soft supplement to the methodology. It is a load-bearing part of the instrument.
This volume is my attempt to build a testing scaffolding for the Laws of the Spirit project — something rigorous enough to be taken seriously as a research program, and personal enough to be used by a small group of people meeting in someone's living room on a Thursday night. That is a hard combination to hold together. I am going to try.
Start here, with Sections 1 and 2. They lay the groundwork — what we mean by testing, and how we will do it. The detailed protocols for individuals, small groups, and formal research come in the sections that follow.
